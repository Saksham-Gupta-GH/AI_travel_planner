\begin{abstract}
Travel applications commonly focus on a single user group and fragmented functions, where package publishing, exploration, booking, and governance are implemented as disconnected modules. This paper presents a Flutter-based mobile and web travel platform that unifies three operational roles (traveler, agent, and admin) in one end-to-end workflow. The core novelty is \textit{preference-aware travel package guidance with role-based workflow orchestration and map-centric interaction}. In the proposed system, travelers discover and filter packages according to intent and budget, agents create and manage location-aware offerings, and administrators supervise operational quality and platform integrity. The platform integrates Firebase Authentication, Cloud Firestore, OpenStreetMap-driven geocoding/map interaction, QR-based booking traceability, and a context-aware AI assistant sidebar for in-app support. We discuss architecture, implementation methodology, and qualitative evaluation from an Advanced Technologies Lab perspective focused on Flutter engineering practices. Results indicate improved workflow continuity, better usability for different stakeholders, and scalable integration of modern mobile technologies without complex infrastructure. The study also highlights deployment trade-offs and future improvements for personalization, optimization, and production readiness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The post-pandemic digital travel ecosystem has accelerated demand for mobile-first systems that support planning, package publishing, and service governance within a single software environment. However, many travel applications are either consumer-only marketplaces or admin-heavy dashboards that do not provide an integrated lifecycle from package creation to traveler engagement and system supervision. This gap is relevant in academic mobile development as students often implement isolated features without cross-role orchestration.

In this Advanced Technologies Lab project, we design and implement a complete travel planning platform using Flutter, with a practical emphasis on multi-role workflows, map-assisted interaction, and scalable backend integration. The system supports three actors: (1) travelers, who explore, filter, and book packages; (2) agents, who publish and manage package inventory; and (3) admins, who supervise users, agents, and bookings. Beyond standard CRUD behavior, the platform introduces map-aware destination interaction and an AI sidebar that can provide contextual guidance from inside the application flow.

The central contribution of this paper is the following novelty statement:
\begin{quote}
\textit{Preference-aware travel package guidance with role-based workflow orchestration and map-centric interaction.}
\end{quote}

This means the application is not only a booking interface. It coordinates the full process: package creation with geospatial context, traveler-side discovery and booking decisions, and administrative oversight of platform operations. The work also demonstrates how Flutter can unify web and mobile targets while maintaining a single engineering codebase.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section II surveys recent literature and tooling trends from 2020--2025. Section III explains the system methodology and architecture. Section IV discusses implementation outcomes and observations. Section V concludes with lessons and future work.

\section{Literature Survey}
Recent research and industry practice between 2020 and 2025 provide a strong foundation for travel-tech systems that combine personalization, geospatial intelligence, and AI-supported interaction.

\subsection{AI and Preference Modeling}
Large language model progress has influenced recommendation interfaces and user guidance channels. Brown \textit{et al.} \cite{brown2020} demonstrated emergent capabilities in few-shot language understanding, while Ouyang \textit{et al.} \cite{ouyang2022} formalized alignment through instruction tuning and preference optimization. The GPT-4 report \cite{gpt42023} and Gemini technical report \cite{gemini2023} show practical multimodal and assistant-oriented paradigms that can be adapted to in-app support rather than replacing domain workflows. In the recommender systems domain, Ricci \textit{et al.} \cite{ricci2022} emphasize context and user intent as essential signals for decision support.

\subsection{Mobile and Cross-Platform Engineering}
Flutter has become a widely adopted framework for cross-platform development, enabling shared UI logic for Android, iOS, and web \cite{flutterdocs}. Firebase services further reduce backend complexity by offering managed authentication, Firestore persistence, and real-time synchronization \cite{firebasedocs}. For educational labs, this stack allows rapid prototyping while still exposing students to production-relevant architecture patterns.

\subsection{Map-Centric Travel Interaction}
Geospatial interfaces improve discoverability and decision confidence in tourism applications. OpenStreetMap and Nominatim services \cite{osm2024,nominatim2024} provide practical geocoding/reverse geocoding support without mandatory paid API tiers. Combined with client-side map renderers such as \texttt{flutter\_map} \cite{fluttermap2025}, developers can implement destination exploration and location capture as first-class interaction elements.

\subsection{Role-Based Governance and Platform Operations}
Role-based access control remains a key software security and process-management principle \cite{nistzero2020}. In travel and e-commerce-like systems, separating responsibilities among buyers, sellers, and administrators improves auditability and operational reliability. Our application adopts this principle by maintaining distinct dashboards, permissions, and workflows per role.

\subsection{Research Gap}
Existing systems and student projects often implement either recommendation or booking, and frequently target only one persona. A practical gap remains in integrating preference-guided discovery, map-based context, AI assistance, and multi-role governance in one coherent Flutter solution. This project addresses that integration gap.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Development Context and Objectives}
The project was implemented in an Advanced Technologies Lab setting, with objectives to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item design a real-world mobile/web product architecture in Flutter;
    \item implement role-specific workflows with shared backend services;
    \item integrate map and geocoding features in user and agent journeys;
    \item include AI-assisted interaction using modern LLM APIs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{System Architecture}
The application follows a layered architecture:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Presentation layer:} Flutter screens and widgets for traveler, agent, and admin workflows.
    \item \textbf{State and flow layer:} provider-based authentication state and role-routing logic.
    \item \textbf{Service layer:} Firebase auth, Firestore data access, geocoding service, AI assistant API service.
    \item \textbf{Data layer:} Cloud Firestore collections for users, packages, bookings, and reviews.
\end{enumerate}

This architecture improves modularity and allowed incremental implementation in lab sprints.

\subsection{Preference-Aware Guidance}
Preference awareness is implemented through user-facing filters and context-driven package presentation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item destination and budget-based filtering;
    \item itinerary/highlight and review visibility on package detail;
    \item contextual AI assistant responses using active screen and role information.
\end{itemize}
This does not yet perform full multi-objective optimization, but it enables guided decision-making aligned with user intent.

\subsection{Map-Centric Interaction Design}
Map usage is integrated into both supply and demand sides:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Traveler side:} world explorer screen with package markers and searchable geospatial discovery.
    \item \textbf{Agent side:} location picker screen with tap-to-select, geocoding, and reverse geocoding.
    \item \textbf{Detail side:} destination map preview inside package details.
\end{itemize}
By placing maps inside core workflows, location context becomes actionable rather than decorative.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Functional Outcomes}
The implemented prototype successfully demonstrates full role-aware execution flow:
\begin{itemize}
    \item users authenticate and are redirected by role;
    \item agents can create and manage location-aware packages;
    \item travelers discover packages through list and map interactions, then book;
    \item admins obtain operational visibility and supervisory controls;
    \item AI sidebar assists users without leaving the current workflow.
\end{itemize}

From a lab perspective, this validates that a single Flutter codebase can deliver a cohesive, multi-stakeholder travel platform with production-relevant building blocks.

\subsection{Usability and Workflow Continuity}
Role-specific dashboards reduced cognitive load by showing only relevant actions. Map-assisted workflows improved destination comprehension for both agents and travelers. The booking history module with QR generation contributed to booking traceability and user trust.

The AI sidebar added conversational support for exploration and task clarification, which can be valuable for novice travelers or first-time users. Integrating this capability directly in-app avoids context switching to external tools.

\subsection{Engineering Learnings in ATL Context}
The project provided practical exposure to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Flutter widget composition and route orchestration;
    \item provider-based state handling;
    \item Firebase auth/data modeling and real-time streams;
    \item API integration using HTTP clients;
    \item map and geocoding services in mobile UX.
\end{itemize}
These outcomes align well with Advanced Technologies Lab objectives for applied, full-stack mobile software development.

\subsection{Limitations}
Current implementation limitations include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item payment details are captured, but no payment gateway settlement is integrated;
    \item explicit GPS current-location and route polylines are not included;
    \item recommendation is preference-aware via filtering and context, but not yet a formal multi-objective optimizer;
    \item scalability and security hardening (e.g., stricter role provisioning) can be further improved for production.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}
Future extensions can include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item hybrid recommendation with ranking models and objective weighting;
    \item real-time navigation and distance-aware route suggestions;
    \item payment gateway integration and stronger transaction audit trails;
    \item analytics-driven personalization and A/B-tested UI refinements.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a Flutter-based travel planning platform developed in an Advanced Technologies Lab setting, emphasizing a practical novelty: \textit{preference-aware travel package guidance with role-based workflow orchestration and map-centric interaction}. Unlike single-role booking demos, the system unifies traveler, agent, and admin journeys under one architecture and demonstrates meaningful integration of maps, cloud backend services, QR-enabled booking tracking, and AI-assisted support.

The project shows that modern cross-platform frameworks can support complex, multi-actor application design without requiring separate mobile/web codebases. Beyond technical implementation, the study highlights a replicable educational model for building real-world software systems in laboratory environments. With planned enhancements in optimization, payments, and geospatial intelligence, the platform can evolve from a robust academic prototype into a deployment-ready travel-tech solution.

\subsection{Units}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use either SI (MKS) or CGS as primary units. (SI units are encouraged.) English units may be used as secondary units (in parentheses). An exception would be the use of English units as identifiers in trade, such as ``3.5-inch disk drive''.

\subsection{\LaTeX-Specific Advice}

Please use ``soft'' (e.g., \verb|\eqref{Eq}|) cross references instead
of ``hard'' references (e.g., \verb|(1)|). That will make it possible
to combine sections, add equations, or change the order of figures or
citations without having to go through the file line by line.

Please don't use the \verb|{eqnarray}| equation environment. Use
\verb|{align}| or \verb|{IEEEeqnarray}| instead. The \verb|{eqnarray}|
environment leaves unsightly spaces around relation symbols.

Please note that the \verb|{subequations}| environment in {\LaTeX}
will increment the main equation counter even when there are no
equation numbers displayed. If you forget that, you might write an
article in which the equation numbers skip from (17) to (20), causing
the copy editors to wonder if you've discovered a new method of
counting.

{\BibTeX} does not work by magic. It doesn't get the bibliographic
data from thin air but from .bib files. If you use {\BibTeX} to produce a
bibliography you must send the .bib files. 

{\LaTeX} can't read your mind. If you assign the same label to a
subsubsection and a table, you might find that Table I has been cross
referenced as Table IV-B3. 

{\LaTeX} does not have precognitive abilities. If you put a
\verb|\label| command before the command that updates the counter it's
supposed to be using, the label will pick up the last counter to be
cross referenced instead. In particular, a \verb|\label| command
should not go before the caption of a figure or a table.

Do not use \verb|\nonumber| inside the \verb|{array}| environment. It
will not stop equation numbers inside \verb|{array}| (there won't be
any anyway) and it might stop a wanted equation number in the
surrounding equation.

\subsection{Some Common Mistakes}\label{SCM}
\begin{itemize}
\item The word ``data'' is plural, not singular.
\item The subscript for the permeability of vacuum $\mu_{0}$, and other common scientific constants, is zero with subscript formatting, not a lowercase letter ``o''.
\item In American English, commas, semicolons, periods, question and exclamation marks are located within quotation marks only when a complete thought or name is cited, such as a title or full quotation. When quotation marks are used, instead of a bold or italic typeface, to highlight a word or phrase, punctuation should appear outside of the quotation marks. A parenthetical phrase or statement at the end of a sentence is punctuated outside of the closing parenthesis (like this). (A parenthetical sentence is punctuated within the parentheses.)
\item A graph within a graph is an ``inset'', not an ``insert''. The word alternatively is preferred to the word ``alternately'' (unless you really mean something that alternates).
\item Do not use the word ``essentially'' to mean ``approximately'' or ``effectively''.
\item In your paper title, if the words ``that uses'' can accurately replace the word ``using'', capitalize the ``u''; if not, keep using lower-cased.
\item Be aware of the different meanings of the homophones ``affect'' and ``effect'', ``complement'' and ``compliment'', ``discreet'' and ``discrete'', ``principal'' and ``principle''.
\item Do not confuse ``imply'' and ``infer''.
\item The prefix ``non'' is not a word; it should be joined to the word it modifies, usually without a hyphen.
\item There is no period after the ``et'' in the Latin abbreviation ``et al.''.
\item The abbreviation ``i.e.'' means ``that is'', and the abbreviation ``e.g.'' means ``for example''.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Identify the Headings}
Headings, or heads, are organizational devices that guide the reader through 
your paper. There are two types: component heads and text heads.

Component heads identify the different components of your paper and are not 
topically subordinate to each other. Examples include Acknowledgments and 
References and, for these, the correct style to use is ``Heading 5''. Use 
``figure caption'' for your Figure captions, and ``table head'' for your 
table title. Run-in heads, such as ``Abstract'', will require you to apply a 
style (in this case, italic) in addition to the style provided by the drop 
down menu to differentiate the head from the text.

Text heads organize the topics on a relational, hierarchical basis. For 
example, the paper title is the primary text head because all subsequent 
material relates and elaborates on this one topic. If there are two or more 
sub-topics, the next level head (uppercase Roman numerals) should be used 
and, conversely, if there are not at least two sub-topics, then no subheads 
should be introduced.

\section*{References}

Please number citations consecutively within brackets \cite{b1}. The 
sentence punctuation follows the bracket \cite{b2}. Refer simply to the reference 
number, as in \cite{b3}---do not use ``Ref. \cite{b3}'' or ``reference \cite{b3}'' except at 
the beginning of a sentence: ``Reference \cite{b3} was the first $\ldots$''

Number footnotes separately in superscripts. Place the actual footnote at 
the bottom of the column in which it was cited. Do not put footnotes in the 
abstract or reference list. Use letters for table footnotes.

Unless there are six authors or more give all authors' names; do not use 
``et al.''. Papers that have not been published, even if they have been 
submitted for publication, should be cited as ``unpublished'' \cite{b4}. Papers 
that have been accepted for publication should be cited as ``in press'' \cite{b5}. 
Capitalize only the first word in a paper title, except for proper nouns and 
element symbols.

For papers published in translation journals, please give the English 
citation first, followed by the original foreign-language citation \cite{b6}.
